Major Accomplishments

Upon taking office in 1921, Harding immediately set to work on implementing his vision for economic recovery and stability. One of his first accomplishments was the establishment of the Bureau of the Budget, a key step in organizing and managing government finances more effectively. He also worked to pass tax cuts that benefited businesses and the wealthy, with the aim of stimulating the economy and encouraging investment. Harding’s administration also implemented protective tariffs to safeguard American industries from foreign competition, and he promoted policies that favored economic growth and business development.

Conflicts

Despite these efforts to foster economic recovery, Harding’s presidency was overshadowed by scandals that severely damaged his reputation. The most infamous of these scandals was the Teapot Dome scandal, in which high-ranking officials in Harding’s administration were implicated in bribery and corruption related to the leasing of federal oil reserves. Additionally, the Veterans Bureau scandal revealed widespread mismanagement and fraud in the care of war veterans. Although Harding was not personally involved in these scandals, they tarnished his presidency and created an atmosphere of distrust in his leadership. Harding's reputation for honesty and integrity was severely damaged, and these scandals became a defining part of his legacy.

Legacy

Despite the scandals, Harding’s presidency also saw positive developments. He was an advocate for civil rights, speaking out against lynching and supporting federal anti-lynching legislation, although he was unable to achieve significant legislative victories in this area. Harding’s leadership during his presidency was marked by a desire to bring stability to a nation recovering from war, but his time in office was ultimately cut short by his sudden death from a heart attack on August 2, 1923. Harding’s death in office meant that he did not have the opportunity to complete his second term or fully realize his political ambitions, leaving a mixed legacy of both accomplishments and controversies.
