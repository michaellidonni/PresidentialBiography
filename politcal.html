Early Politics

Harding’s entry into politics was influenced by his early success in journalism and his growing interest in public service. He joined the Republican Party and began his political career in Ohio, serving in the state Senate and eventually becoming the Governor of Ohio. During his time in Ohio politics, Harding gained a reputation for being a moderate and pragmatic leader. His political platform was largely focused on pro-business policies, advocating for limited government intervention in the economy, and emphasizing the importance of economic growth and stability.

Election

In 1920, Harding ran for president of the United States, campaigning on a promise of "return to normalcy" after the turmoil of World War I. The United States was facing significant economic challenges following the war, and Harding’s message resonated with voters who were eager to return to a sense of stability. His opponent, James M. Cox, ran on a platform that included support for the League of Nations, but Harding’s call for a more isolationist approach and his focus on domestic economic recovery led to a landslide victory. Harding secured 404 electoral votes, while Cox only garnered 127, marking a decisive shift in American politics toward conservatism and a preference for isolationism in foreign affairs.
